Implementation

1. Agents

In our model, there are three types of agent roles: villager, mafia, and detective. We have chosen to limit the roles to three to keep the model relatively simple. The roles of Mafia and Villager are essential for the game, but to further incorporate logic, the role of detective has been added. Each role has its own characteristics and abilities:

  • Villager is a passive role, that is only able to participate in voting during the day phase.
  • Mafia is a more knowledgable and powerful role. They know who the other mafiosi are and are able to murder other players during the night phase.
  • The detective is able to inspect any player's role during the night, being the only role that gets to expand its knowledge in a different way from conversations. The detective is part of the townfolk, meaning he/she works together with the villagers.

During the day phase, all agents have the ability to interact with each other before voting. This interaction allows them to gather information and identify potential mafia members who may be acting suspiciously. To determine the likelihood of agents initiating a conversation, we assign each of them a sociability stat (s), randomly sampled from a uniform distribution between 0 and 1. At the beginning of the day phase, another random number between 0 and 1 is sampled. If an agent's s is greater than or equal to this number, they are marked as wanting to engage in a conversation. Conversations can only occur between two agents if both of them are marked as wanting to talk. In the actual game of Mafia, player conversations provide valuable information as participants observe human behavior cues such as stuttering, excessive blinking, or signs of lying to identify suspicious individuals. Additionally, the mafia attempts to manipulate other agents into voting for innocent townsfolk instead of themselves. However, in our model, we limit conversations to two players, allowing them to assess whether the other agent is displaying suspicious behavior or not. The subsequent sections will provide further details on the implementation of suspicion and how agents behave when they suspect another agent.

2. Knowledge and Rules

2.1 Knowledge Types

There are three types of knowledge we represent in the model, namely:
  • Role-related knowledge: Agent i is a villager, mafiosi or detective = vi, mi, di, respectively
  • Knowledge of that agent i is acting suspiciously: susi
  • Knowledge of that agent i has been killed: deadi

2.2 Rules

The following rules represent the logical rules of our model. With rule 1 through 4 dealing with how agents can obtain knowledge and rule 5 and 6 deal with how the agents use it in voting.
  1. As mentioned above, suspicion of an agent can occur during conversation. To elaborate on this, mafia are always considered to be acting suspiciously as this is inherent to their role: mi → susi. Note that it is also possible for other roles to act suspicious, which will be explained in Section 3.
  2. Agents get suspicious of each other based on the conversations they have with each other: (agenti talks to agentj ∧ susj) → Kisusj.
  3. Agents get to know each other's suspicions through conversation: (agenti talks to agentj ∧ Kjsusk) → KiKjsusk.
  4. The detective can get to know whether another agent is Mafia with his special ability:
    1. (di ∧ inspect agentj ∧ mj) → Kimj
    2. (di ∧ inspect agentj ∧ ¬mj)→ Ki¬mj
  5. Mafia murder and vote on (in order)
    1. Agents that they know are suspicious of them: KiKjsusi ∧ ¬ deadj
    2. A random agent
  6. Townfolk vote on (in order):
    1. Agents that they are suspicous of: Kisusj ∧ ¬ deadj.
    2. Agents they know other agents are suspicous of: KiKjsusk ∧ ¬ deadk. We consider this as a second, less trustworthy type of knowledge as it is the agents have not conversated with the suspicious agent themselves.
    3. A random agent

In our simulation, it is important to note that a voting tie can occur when using rules 5 and 6. To address this situation, we have chosen to randomly resolve the tie and eliminate a player. This decision was made to prevent the simulation from getting stuck in a stalemate, where all agents continuously vote for the same player and ties occur in a repetitive loop. Randomly resolving ties is also a recognized approach in real-life Mafia games to break voting deadlocks [2].

3. The Kripke Model

To model the agents knowledge and what they consider to be possible, we use a kripke model [3]. This is a mathematical structure used in modal logic to represent the knowledge and belief states of agents in a system. It consists of a set of possible worlds, each with its own interpretation of propositions, and a set of accessibility relations that describe the relationships between worlds and the agents' beliefs about those relationships. The Kripke model provides a framework for reasoning about the logical properties of knowledge, belief, and other modalities within a given system and is thus very suitable for our multi agent system of Mafia.

3.1 Initialization

Our Kripke model represents the different combinations of roles and suspicions in the game scenario. It is initialized with every possible permutation of the chosen roles, along with possible combinations for which agents can be suspicious. As mentioned previously, mafia is always considered to be acting suspiciously. Alongside this however, we mark every other role as suspicious with a probabilitiy of p. This means that it is indeed possible for every single agent to act suspiciously, which results in various extra worlds in the intialization. To examplify, say we have a game with two villagers, two mafiosi, and one detective. Considering the possible combinations of roles, we have a total of 5!/(2!2!1!) = 30 different role assignments for the players. Additionally, we have the concept of suspicions, where each player can be suspicious or not suspicious. Since the mafia is always suspicious, we don't consider different possibilities for their suspicions. However, the villagers and detective can be suspicious or not suspicious, resulting in 2^3 = 8 different combinations of suspicions for these players. Therefore, in our model, we have a total of 30 * 8 = 240 different worlds in the Kripke model, each representing a unique combination of roles and suspicions for the players. As for the relation, we consider the following:

  • Every agent knows their own role meaning that they can only distinguish between worlds where the their role is assigned in the same way:: ∀((vx → Kxvx) ∧ (mx → Kxmx ) ∧ (dx → Kxdx ))
  • Mafia knows who the other mafia is, meaning that they can only distinguish between worlds where the mafia role is assigned in the same way: ∀x(mx → ∀y(my → Kxmy ))

3.2 Formal model

Kripke model M with <S, π, R1, R2,..., Rn>
  • A set of states S = {si|i is a permutation of the roles' distribution
  • A truth assignment π which assigns a truth value to all propositions P where π(si)(rolej) = t ↔ ij = role with role ∈ {v,m,d}
  • A set of relations for each agent i: Ri = {(si, sj)|} where for each Rj⊆ SxS such that
    1. Rj = {(u,v) ∈ SxS | π(u)(rolej) = t ↔ π(v)(rolej)=t} where j ∈ Agents
    2. Rj = {(u,v) ∈ SxS | (π(u)(mj) = t and π(u)(mk)) = t ↔ π(v)(mj) = t and π(v)(mk) = t}; where j, k ∈ Agents
